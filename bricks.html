Bangla bricks: constellations of
weathering

“A focus on life-processes requires us to attend not to materiality as such but to the fluxes and flows of materials. We are obliged … to follow these flows, tracing the paths of form-generation, wherever they may lead”.¹

Following the life cycle of the Bangla brick from sediment to clay, from clay to brick and from brick to sediment, reveals how monsoons are embedded within built environments. Perceiving bricks as constellations of weathering highlights the plethora of agencies that co-produce urban materialities and troubles perceived dichotomies between society and meteorological forces.

Weather and bricks

From its earliest beginnings brickmaking has been bound up with the weather. By regulating the microclimates of human habitation, bricks mediate relations with weather and climate. As well as being reactive to weather, bricks are entangled with weather through their production. Historically, brickmaking was a seasonal activity due to its reliance on the heat of the sun. Relations between weather and brickmaking are still apparent in the monsoonal terrain of Bangladesh. During the wet season frequent rain, high humidity and reduced sunlight impede the drying and firing of bricks. Due to these monsoonal fluctuations, brick fields only operate during drier months, the season of production determined by the monsoon.

Bangla bricks

Brick fields are immediately apparent on visiting Dhaka, they proliferate along river banks surrounding the city, their towering chimneys emerging from the low-lying riverine terrain. Bricks form a ubiquitous, ever-present part of the urban landscape; their visibility and mobility an indicator of rampant urbanisation. Brick making in Bangladesh has grown exponentially to supply the booming infrastructure and construction industry. Although brick kilns are prevalent throughout Bangladesh they have mushroomed around Dhaka, one of the world’s largest cities. The continued growth of the city is contingent on the expansion of the brick industry with consequences for humans and nonhumans alike.

Sediment to clay: geological
mobilities

Bangladesh bricks are reliant on clay-rich soils extracted from the alluvial floodplains of the delta. Clay, the essential element for bricks, is almost always formed through weathering; emerging from erosive movements of wind, water and rain. The Bengal delta is formed from alluvial materials, mostly derived from the Himalayas. Through weathering, sedimentary rocks are transported and deposited by seasonal monsoonal flows, forming the clay-rich soils that become Bangla bricks. The brick sector in Bangladesh consumes an estimated forty-five million tons of clay every year, as farmers sell their clay-rich top soil to the brick kilns. Due to the speed of urbanisation, removal of topsoil for brickmaking is occurring at a rate that cannot compete with geological cycles.

Clay to brick: atmospheric
mobilities

Brick making operates around a monsoonal cycle, beginning in November and ending in April before the first monsoon rains arrive. Brick kilns churn relentlessly in the dry season, dependent on the labour of seasonal migrants. Plumes of black smoke, blankets of thick smog and clouds of brick dust flux with the seasons, emissions peaking in December and January. Dhaka has some of the most polluted air in the world, with brickfields responsible for up to forty percent of ambient PM 2.5 pollution. These tiny airborne particles cause respiratory conditions, hinder plant growth, pollute soils and erode building surfaces. Pollutants also become entangled with regional atmospheric circulations, contributing to changing monsoon weather patterns.

Brick to sediment: hydrological
mobilities

After firing, bricks are classified and allocated for use in buildings and infrastructure. A large proportion are over fired so are broken into small pieces called pickets. Crushed bricks form a base layer for nearly all paved roads in Bangladesh. Roads are typically constructed on top of embankments to raise them above wet season flood levels. Although roads create new geographies and lines of relation, they also obstruct flows of water and sediment by imposing inflexible linear forms into a fluid landscape. Bangladesh roads deteriorate during the rainy season, pummelled by traffic, wind and rain. Through weathering and disintegration brick returns to sediment, creating seasonal cycles of maintenance, fuelling the never-ending demand for brick.

Brick-weather entanglements

Following the materiality of the brick reveals the multiple ways in which the monsoon is enmeshed within lived environments in Bangladesh. Bricks come into being not through human agency alone, but through entanglements of geology, weather and human energies. Weather contributes to the emergence of bricks, their production, use, and dissolution. Far from being a disconnected backdrop to social life, meteorological forces are coconstitutive of social worlds, moving through them and the materials that compose them.
