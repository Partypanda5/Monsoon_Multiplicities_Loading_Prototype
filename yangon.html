Yangon: monsoonal-geologic life

“Just as Deleuze and Guattari ventured that all history is geohistory, so too can we add that all social formations are geosocial formations and that all life is geologic life”.¹

Here we explore Myanmar through the idea of the geological monsoon. This idea suggests the collapse of distinctions between the substance and agency of the subterranean earth and the substance and agency of the atmosphere, or rather of the co-constitution of one by the other. We propose that socio-political life in Myanmar has been closely bound up with the mutual intra-actions between geology and the monsoon over the course of its history.

Monsoonal tectonics

Myanmar is one of the most tectonically active regions on earth and lies between two interlinked monsoonal systems. It originated as part of ancient supercontinent Gondwana millions of years ago, when tectonic slivers and later the Indian plate broke off and edged slowly northwards. These plates eventually collided with Eurasia, forming Indochina and the Himalayas and rotating Southeast Asia through 70o. This movement set up enormous shearing stresses along the Sagaing Fault that continue today. At the centre of Myanmar in the rain shadows of the Arakan Yoma to the west and the Shan Plateau to the east, both formed by this tectonic history, lies the Dry Zone that was the centre of power for much of Myanmar’s history

Monsoonal societies

Myanmar’s earliest known urban cultures built walled cities in the Dry Zone and on the Gulf of Martaban. Pagan, a brick-walled city on the banks of the Ayeyarwady River, became the capital of the Pagan Dynasty, one of the great kingdoms of Southeast Asia. It was at its zenith during the Medieval Climate Anomaly (900-1300 CE) when the Inter Tropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ) lay along a northerly latitude and long La Niña conditions prevailed. Strong monsoons and high rainfall in Southeast Asia spurred agricultural production and brought wealth and political stability. When the ITCZ shifted southwards, monsoons weakened and prolonged droughts and famine followed. This put Pagan under severe ecological and political strain and competing centres of power emerged in the Dry Zone and Lower Myanmar.

Top: A map of Myanmar showing the relationship between the mountains and highlands formed by its tectonic history, rainfall, and historical centres of power. The Dry Zone in the centre of the country, positioned in the rain shadows of the Arakan Yoma to the west and the Shan Plateau to the east, is clearly visible. It was here that Myanmar’s centre of power resided for most of its history.

British Rangoon

In 1755, King Alaungpaya of the Dry Zone Konbaung Dynasty conquered the cities of Lower Myanmar and laid out a new settlement at Dagon at the base of the Shwedagon Pagoda. It soon grew into a flourishing port. In 1824 it was the target of British assault during the first Anglo-Burmese War, and again in the second in 1852 when it was completely destroyed. On the site of Alaungpaya’s Dagon, the British built a new city they called Rangoon. During colonial rule (1852-1947), it was the commercial, financial and administrative capital of British Burma and, for a time, one of the busiest port cities in Asia.

British interests in Burma focused on the extraction of the country’s geomonsoonal resources. Teak was logged for shipbuilding and railway construction and Burma became the biggest exporter of rice in the world. In 1889 the Glasgow based Rangoon Oil Company sunk wells at Yenangyaung in the Dry Zone, becoming the British Empire’s most important source of oil until Burmah Oil purchased concessions in Iran in 1905. Colonial companies were floated to mine silver and lead at the Namtu-Baldwin mining complex in Shan State, tungsten at Mawchi in Kayah State and Myanmar’s famous rubies at Mogok. Jade was of no interest to the British, but it seized control of the jade mines in Kachin State in 1885 and set up regulations to control them.

Jade mining

Spectacularly rich deposits of jade are found in Myanmar in Kachin State. After Myanmar’s independence in 1947 they fell under the control of the Kachin Independence Army (KIA) and fuelled the hopes of struggles for Kachin independence. Ceasefire arrangements in the 1990s transferred the deposits into the control of Myanmar’s military junta. Military-owned conglomerates and private businesses close to the ruling elite came to dominate the jade economy, replacing artisanal mining with industrial scale operations that radically altered the environment and produced astounding levels of wealth. Jade mining is seasonal as mines cannot be worked during monsoon months. However unregistered jade diggers known as yemase (meaning ‘unwashed stones’) eke out a dangerous living mining the tailings created by industrialised mining operations.

Jade networks

China is the main market for Myanmar’s jade and there are extensive trading networks for the stone between Myanmar and China. While high quality jade is sold at the official gem emporium in Naypyidaw, lower grade stones are sold at jade markets in Mandalay and Yangon. The biggest of these is the Mahar Aung Myay market in Mandalay, which hosts up to 40,000 buyers and sellers daily, although trade slows during the monsoon months. From here jade is legally and illegally dissolved into Chinese markets, facilitated by a network of agents, infrastructures and material and digital flows. The primary routes are through Ruili via Muse the capital of Shan State, or through a number of small towns along the Chinese border via Myitkyina, the capital of Kachin State.

Jade real estate

From the mid 1990s onwards, Yangon experienced a construction boom. While jade was transported across the border into China, much of the wealth it generated flowed into infrastructure and real estate in Yangon. Unsurprisingly, many of Yangon’s shopping malls, banks, hotels and other real estate ventures are owned by the same individuals who exploit Myanmar’s jade resources. It is an open secret that jade mining has provided the seed capital for banks, businesses and real estate development in Yangon, folding geo-monsoonal wealth into the city’s glittering new edifices and opulent interiors.

Expanding relations with China

Accelerated development of Yangon after 2010 put enormous strain on the city’s infrastructure and raised concerns about its deteriorating urban environment. In 2018 the regional government launched a project to develop a large tract of agricultural land west of central Yangon into an urban industrial district that will eventually extend south to the Gulf of Martaban. The infrastructural contracts for this development were awarded to a Chinese state owned company. New Yangon, as it is called, is the southernmost node of a forked China-Myanmar Economic Corridor, linking Yangon to a 2,380 km oil and gas pipeline from Myanmar’s Rakhine State to China’s inland megacity, Chongqing. Thus Myanmar’s geomonsoonal resources are being tied ever closer to Chinese markets and agendas.
